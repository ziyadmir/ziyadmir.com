Is It AI Slop? The Truth About How I Write

July 5, 2025

Here's an uncomfortable truth: 98% of people who land on this blog take one look and think "AI slop." They see the writing style, the punctuation patterns, the structure, and their brain immediately categorizes it as machine-generated garbage. Click. Gone.

The other 2%? They actually read the content. And here's where it gets interesting.

My Actual Writing Process

Let me pull back the curtain on how these posts actually come to life. It's not what you think.

The Real Process

1
Stream of Consciousness
I open Notes, turn on voice dictation, and just talk. No filter, no editing, just pure thought dump about topics I've spent 15+ years thinking about.
2
Raw Ideas Flow
Technical concepts mix with half-baked visions, deep intuitions collide with current feelings. It's messy, personal, and completely authentic.
3
AI as Translator
I feed this raw stream to an AI model. Not to generate ideas, but to organize and clarify what I've already said. Think of it as a really good editor, not a writer.

The AI isn't creating content—it's making my existing thoughts accessible. And here's the kicker: even people with minds very similar to mine understand my ideas better after the AI has organized them.

The Voice Experiment

This bothered me. If AI was helping communicate my ideas, shouldn't it at least sound like me? So I ran an experiment.

I took a 3,000-word personal essay (not published online) and used it as a style guide. Then I had AI rewrite some of my posts "in my voice." The results? You can see them in the AI Voice Experiment series on this blog.

The Surprising Results: When I showed both versions to people—standard AI output vs. AI mimicking my voice—the audience split 50/50. Half preferred the clarity of standard AI writing. Half loved the personality of my mimicked voice but found it harder to extract specific details.

The Slop Paradox

So is this blog "AI slop"? That depends entirely on your definition.

If slop means "generated by AI," then yes, technically these words were assembled by a machine. But if slop means "generic, thoughtless content with no original ideas," then absolutely not. Every concept here comes from decades of experience, deep thinking, and personal insight.

The AI is a tool, like a word processor or a grammar checker. It doesn't generate my thoughts—it helps me communicate them to you more clearly.

The Hidden Advantage Problem

Right now, using AI effectively is a competitive advantage. People who can leverage these tools have a massive productivity edge. So there's an incentive to hide AI usage, to make it indistinguishable from human writing.

But this creates a trust problem. If you can't tell whether you're reading human thoughts or machine generation, how do you evaluate the content? How do you know if there's a real person with real experience behind the words?

The Future: Explicit AI Assistants

Here's my prediction: we're heading toward a world where everyone has AI assistants, and we'll want them to be clearly identifiable.

Think about executive assistants today. When they send emails on behalf of their boss, they often sign with their own name. "Sarah, on behalf of John." We don't call those emails "slop"—we understand the communication structure.

The Coming Shift

In the future, you might interact with multiple versions of me: the raw human Ziyad, my technical writing AI, my scheduling AI, my research AI. Each would have a distinct voice, making it clear who (or what) you're communicating with.

This isn't deception—it's transparency. When AIs are talking to other AIs on our behalf, we'll need clear delineation between human-generated and AI-generated content.

Why This Matters

The "AI slop" label is too simplistic. It lumps together:

  • Thoughtless, SEO-optimized content farms
  • Carefully crafted human ideas with AI assistance
  • Genuine expertise filtered through machine translation
  • Complete AI fabrications with no human insight

These are fundamentally different things. By dismissing all AI-touched content as "slop," we miss out on valuable ideas from people who simply use different tools to communicate.

The Challenge to You

Next time you land on a blog and your brain screams "AI slop," pause for a second. Ask yourself:

  • Are the ideas original and insightful?
  • Does the content reflect real experience and expertise?
  • Is the AI adding clarity or generating filler?
  • Would you dismiss the same ideas if they were poorly formatted but obviously human-written?

I'm not asking you to love AI-assisted writing. I'm asking you to evaluate content based on the value of the ideas, not the tools used to express them.

Because at the end of the day, what matters more: perfect human prose saying nothing, or AI-polished thoughts that change how you see the world?

Judge for yourself. The ideas are mine. The organization is AI's. The value? That's for you to determine.
