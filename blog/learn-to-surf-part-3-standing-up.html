You can catch the wave. You're riding it. But you're still lying down on your board like a confused seal. The next challenge—and it's a big one—is figuring out how to get to your feet.

Here's the thing no one tells you: catching the wave is only half the battle. The popup—that magical moment where you transform from prone to standing—is where most beginners get stuck. And I mean really stuck.

The Perfect Popup (That You Can't Do Yet)

In surf videos, the popup looks effortless. One smooth motion: hands flat on the board, explosive push-up, feet land perfectly parallel to the stringer, knees bent, arms out for balance. Done. You're surfing.

In reality? My first attempts looked more like a drunk person trying to do yoga on a moving platform. And that's being generous.

The Physical Reality Check: The popup requires strength, power, flexibility, and coordination—all at the same time, all while the ocean is moving beneath you. If you're missing any of these elements, you're going to struggle.

Why Your Body Won't Cooperate

After countless failed attempts, I've identified the main physical barriers:

  • Ankle flexibility: Can't get your feet flat and parallel to the board? That's your ankles. Mine are so tight that I end up on my toes, not my feet.
  • Hip mobility: The popup requires you to bring your feet forward in one motion. Tight hips make this nearly impossible.
  • Core strength: You need explosive power to push up and control to land balanced.
  • Muscle memory: Your body has never done this movement before. It's completely foreign.

The Slower Alternative: Knee-Through Method

Here's what I discovered today: there's another way. Instead of the explosive popup, you can use a knee-through technique:

  1. Push up to a plank position
  2. Bring one knee forward between your hands
  3. Plant that foot flat on the board
  4. Use that leg to push yourself up to standing

It's slower, less elegant, but it works. And right now, working is all that matters.

Today's Breakthrough: Using the knee-through method, I finally stood up on multiple waves. Were they pretty? No. Did I feel like a "real" surfer? Not really. But I was standing on a moving surfboard in the ocean, and that's progress.

The Bad Habits Dilemma

The scary part is wondering: am I reinforcing bad habits? Every surf instructor emphasizes the importance of learning the "proper" popup. By taking this slower route, am I setting myself up for problems later?

Maybe. But here's my thinking: I'd rather be standing imperfectly on waves than lying down perfectly. You can't learn to surf without actually standing up and riding waves. The feeling of being upright, finding your balance, reading the wave face—these are all crucial skills that you can only develop while standing.

The Path Forward

I'm treating this as a progression, not a permanent solution:

  • Short term: Use the knee-through to actually surf waves and build confidence
  • Meanwhile: Work on ankle flexibility, hip mobility, and explosive strength on land
  • Long term: Gradually transition to a faster, more fluid popup as my body adapts
The Reality: Your body will tell you what it can and can't do. Listen to it. Work with what you have while training for what you want. Progress isn't always linear, and that's okay.

What Actually Matters

Here's what I realized today while finally standing on those 4-foot waves: perfect form is a luxury for later. Right now, it's about:

  • Building the neural pathways of balance and wave reading
  • Getting comfortable with the feeling of speed
  • Learning to shift weight and steer
  • Most importantly: having fun and staying motivated

The perfect popup will come with time, flexibility work, and thousands of repetitions. But for now, I'm choosing function over form. I'm choosing to actually surf, even if it's not pretty.

Because at the end of the day, no one remembers how you got to your feet. They remember that you did.
