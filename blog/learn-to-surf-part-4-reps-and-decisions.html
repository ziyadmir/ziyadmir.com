Part 4: Reps Over Reputation - Why Volume Beats Perfection

July 5, 2025

Surfing Learning Practice

If the first three parts of this series compressed into one instruction, it would be: take more waves. The fastest beginner progress comes from abandoning the need to look good. Surfing rewards attempts. Every paddle, missed wave, wobble, and wipeout gives your body a slightly better map of timing and balance.

Reputation is the enemy

Caring how you look makes you selective too early. You wait for the perfect wave. You skip the iffy one because someone might be watching. You spend the session protecting an image instead of collecting data. I catch myself doing this in other parts of my life too; the water just makes it impossible to hide.

Volume matters because waves vary. You cannot learn the pattern from one ideal rep — you need messy repetitions across slightly different conditions, so your body learns to adjust in real time instead of executing one memorized move that only works in one situation.

Two habits worth building

First, decide fast. A committed paddle for a mediocre wave teaches you more than a hesitant half-paddle for a good one, and hesitation usually costs more than choosing imperfectly. Second, review one thing per session, not fifteen things per attempt. The body can only absorb so much instruction at once, and a single correction repeated all session actually sticks.

The mental model that transfers, in surfing and everywhere else I have tried it: the beginner who is willing to look bad for longer usually gets good faster.

That is the series. If you are starting from zero, Part 1 is the place to begin — and the only instruction that really matters is the first one: leave the water wanting to come back.
