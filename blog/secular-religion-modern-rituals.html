The Secular Religion of the West: Ritual Without God

July 6, 2025

Culture Ritual Society

Religion never only provided belief. It provided rhythm, ceremony, moral language, communal identity — a way to make ordinary life feel connected to something larger. Secular culture rejected the theology and then, mostly without noticing, rebuilt the functions.

Once you see this, it's everywhere. Graduations, conferences, protests, wellness routines, fandoms, national ceremonies, product launches. Anyone who has sat through a company all-hands has felt the liturgical structure: the gathering, the recitation of shared values, the celebration of the faithful, the call to renewed commitment. Nobody calls it liturgy. It is obviously liturgy.

Ritual is coordination technology

The mental model I find most useful here: rituals are coordination technology. They create shared attention, shared emotion, shared timing, and shared symbols — the raw inputs a group needs to act like a group. Humans don't stop needing those inputs when they stop believing in God. So when formal religion recedes, the job doesn't disappear. It gets inherited by whatever institutions are standing nearby: companies, universities, sports leagues, political movements, the internet.

Some of those institutions do the job thoughtfully. Many produce what I'd call thin rituals — forms that mimic meaning without carrying much of it. A thin ritual has the gathering and the applause but no real demands, no confession, nothing at stake. You can usually feel the difference from inside, even when you can't articulate it. The thick version changes you a little. The thin version just fills an hour.

What the ritual makes legitimate

The most revealing question to ask of any ritual, sacred or secular, is what it makes legitimate. Every ritual is an argument, performed rather than stated: this deserves reverence, these people belong, this behavior is admirable, this story should be remembered. Watch what gets repeated, who is allowed to speak, what earns applause, and which version of the group's history is being made durable. That tells you more about an institution's actual values than anything it publishes.

This is also why I'm skeptical of institutions that claim to be purely rational. They keep inventing ritual forms anyway — offsites, launch events, award ceremonies, memorials — because the coordination problem doesn't go away just because you've stopped naming it. The choice was never ritual versus no ritual. It was ritual designed with some self-awareness versus ritual that accumulates by accident.

The secular world did not escape ritual. It redistributed ritual into new containers, and mostly stopped checking what the containers are teaching.
