The Evolution of Tech Leadership: How Senior ICs and Managers Are Reshaping Organizations

July 5, 2025

Meta just laid off thousands of middle managers. Google's been quietly "flattening" for years. Amazon's cutting management layers. While it's tempting to see this as the end of management, it's actually something more interesting—an evolution of how leadership works in tech.

As technical complexity explodes, we're seeing a beautiful convergence: Senior Individual Contributors (ICs) are taking on more leadership responsibilities, while the best managers are becoming more technical. It's not about replacement—it's about finding a new equilibrium that serves everyone better.

Recent Management Layoffs:
• Meta: 11,000+ employees, primarily middle management (2023)
• Amazon: 18,000+ with focus on management layers (2023)
• Google: 12,000+ emphasizing "organizational efficiency" (2023)
• Microsoft: 10,000+ targeting redundant management roles (2023)

The Complexity Challenge We All Face

Let's acknowledge something we all know: modern software systems have become incredibly complex. Whether you're a manager or an IC, we're all grappling with architectures that involve:

  • Hundreds of services written in different languages
  • Dozens of data stores with different consistency models
  • Multiple orchestration layers (Kubernetes, service mesh, etc.)
  • AI/ML pipelines with their own deployment complexities
  • Real-time streaming architectures

This complexity creates a challenge for everyone. Managers who've been away from code struggle to make informed technical decisions. Senior ICs who understand the technology deeply may lack the context for business trade-offs. The solution isn't to eliminate either role—it's to bring them closer together.

A New Collaborative Dynamic

Watch any successful technical planning meeting at a FAANG company today. You'll see something beautiful: senior staff engineers presenting architectures, principal engineers providing technical challenge, distinguished engineers offering historical context, and managers asking crucial questions about resources, timelines, and business impact.

This isn't hierarchy breaking down—it's collaboration emerging. The best teams are finding ways to leverage everyone's strengths: technical depth from ICs, strategic thinking from managers, and increasingly, both from everyone.

Traditional Manager vs Senior IC Leadership

Aspect Traditional Approach Evolved Approach
Technical Decisions Manager decides with team input Shared decision-making based on expertise
Architecture Manager approves, IC proposes Collaborative design with clear ownership
Code Reviews Process-focused review Substance-focused with shared responsibility

Roadmap Business vs. technical split Joint ownership of feasible value delivery

Why We Need to Evolve Leadership Models

The traditional separation of management and technical work made sense in predictable environments. But software development is different, and we're all learning that effective leadership here requires:

  • Every project is unique: You're not manufacturing widgets; you're solving novel problems
  • Technical debt compounds: Bad architectural decisions made by non-technical managers haunt teams for years
  • Speed requires expertise: You can't "coordinate" your way through a complex debugging session
  • Innovation needs depth: Breakthrough insights come from deep technical understanding, not process optimization

The Emergence of Hybrid Leadership

What's exciting is that we're not seeing replacement but evolution. The most effective leaders—whether they started as ICs or managers—are developing hybrid skills:

  • Deep technical expertise to make correct decisions
  • System-wide thinking to see beyond individual components
  • Communication skills to align teams
  • Mentorship abilities to grow others
  • Strategic vision to guide long-term direction

The key insight: leadership in tech isn't about choosing between technical and people skills—it's about combining them in new ways.

The New Equation

The winning formula: Technical Expertise + People Leadership + Business Acumen

The most successful organizations are investing in developing all three capabilities in their leaders, regardless of whether they come from IC or management backgrounds.

What This Means for Our Organizations

More Collaborative Structures

We're moving toward networks of expertise rather than rigid hierarchies. Engineers, senior ICs, and managers form fluid teams based on project needs, with leadership shifting based on context.

True Dual Track Parity

The IC track is achieving real parity with management—not as a consolation prize, but as an equally valuable path. Principal engineers at top companies earn what directors earn because both roles are essential.

Context-Based Leadership

Leadership rotates based on expertise and context. The distributed systems expert leads the infrastructure overhaul. The manager with customer relationships leads the client engagement. Everyone contributes their strengths.

Integrated Development

The best organizations offer both technical mentorship and career development, recognizing that engineers need both to thrive. It's not either/or—it's both/and.

Steel-Manning the Great Manager

Let's be clear: great managers do exist, and they create enormous value. The best managers excel at functions that will remain critical:

Strategic Problem Selection

Great managers have a sixth sense for what problems are worth solving. They can look at a sea of possibilities and identify the one that, if solved, unlocks disproportionate value. They understand market dynamics, user needs, and business constraints in ways that pure technologists sometimes miss.

Team Building as an Art

The best managers don't just hire—they craft teams. They understand that you need the brilliant architect, the execution machine, the creative maverick, and the stabilizing force. They know how personalities mesh, how to balance seniority, and when to hire for potential versus experience. This remains irreplaceable human judgment.

Political Capital and Influence

Perhaps most critically, excellent managers navigate organizational politics to secure resources. They know how to:

  • Frame technical work in business terms that executives understand
  • Build alliances with other teams for shared goals
  • Time requests when budgets are available
  • Present failures as learning opportunities, not disasters
  • Shield their team from organizational dysfunction

This "influence without authority" remains crucial. Someone needs to convince the CFO why you need 10 more engineers, or persuade the product team that technical debt reduction is worth prioritizing.

The Integration Layer

Great managers serve as the translation layer between technical reality and business strategy. They can explain to engineers why a seemingly boring feature matters for the company's survival, and explain to executives why a seemingly simple request will take six months.

Why These Functions Persist

These management skills become even more valuable in the agentic future:

  • Problem Selection: With AI agents at our disposal, choosing the right problems becomes crucial. Great managers ensure we're solving what matters.
  • Team Composition: Balancing human expertise with AI capabilities requires deep judgment about when we need human creativity versus AI efficiency.
  • Resource Acquisition: Whether it's headcount, compute credits, or bounty budgets, someone needs to secure resources and make the case for investment.
  • Strategic Translation: As systems get more complex, we need leaders who can bridge technical reality with business strategy.

The Optimal Future Model

The most successful organizations will pair:

Technical Leaders (often senior ICs) who guide implementation and architecture
Strategic Leaders (often managers) who identify opportunities and secure resources
Hybrid Leaders (increasingly common) who can do both

The magic happens when these roles collaborate rather than compete.

The Evolution of Core Functions

Rather than disappearing, traditional management functions are evolving to better serve modern tech organizations:

  • Hiring and recruiting: Becomes about finding rare human expertise that complements AI
  • Performance management: Shifts to measuring impact and innovation, not activity
  • Budget and resource allocation: Evolves into credit management and ROI optimization
  • Cross-functional coordination: Focuses on aligning human and AI efforts across teams

The key shift: these functions become collaborative efforts between technical and strategic leaders, creating stronger outcomes than either could achieve alone.

Why This Trend Will Accelerate

AI Creates New Opportunities

As AI handles routine coordination, both managers and ICs can focus on higher-value work. Managers can spend more time on strategy and team development. ICs can tackle more complex technical challenges. Everyone wins.

Complexity Demands Collaboration

Systems aren't getting simpler. Every year brings new technologies, frameworks, and architectural patterns. This complexity requires deeper partnership between those who understand the technology and those who understand the business.

Competition for Integrated Leaders

Companies that develop both technical and leadership skills in their people—regardless of title—attract the best talent. Those clinging to rigid role definitions lose out to more adaptive organizations.

The Connection to Agentic Systems

This evolution accelerates when we consider the future of work I've described in previous posts. In a world of credit-based resource allocation, both senior ICs and evolved managers will need to orchestrate human and AI resources together.

Think about it: when teams have compute credits instead of just headcount, and can spin up AI agents to execute ideas, we need leaders who understand:

  • Which tasks AI can handle effectively
  • Where human expertise is still crucial
  • How to architect systems that blend both
  • The technical trade-offs of different approaches

This is why the evolution is so important. Leaders who combine technical understanding with strategic thinking will thrive. Those who cling to only one dimension—whether pure technical or pure management—will struggle.

The Bounty System Connection

This evolution accelerates with the bounty-based economy I've outlined. When AI agents can recognize they need human help and post bounties for specific expertise, who determines:

  • Whether the AI's assessment is correct?
  • What skill level is actually needed?
  • If the bounty price matches the complexity?
  • Whether to solve it internally or post externally?

The leaders who succeed will be those who can evaluate both technical feasibility and business value—regardless of whether they started as ICs or managers.

The New Leadership Stack

Senior ICs → Direct both human engineers and AI agents
Credit Allocation → Based on technical judgment, not politics
Bounty Decisions → Require deep understanding of problems
Result: Integrated leadership becomes the key differentiator

The Path Forward

For engineers: embrace leadership opportunities while maintaining technical excellence. The future rewards those who can do both.

For managers: lean into technical learning. You don't need to become a coding expert, but you need to understand the landscape deeply enough to make informed decisions.

For companies: invest in developing hybrid leaders. Create paths for ICs to gain business acumen and for managers to deepen technical knowledge. Build cultures where both are valued.

The Bottom Line

We're witnessing a beautiful evolution in tech leadership. It's not about ICs replacing managers or managers becoming obsolete. It's about both roles evolving to meet the challenges of modern software development. The future belongs to leaders who can bridge technical and strategic thinking—regardless of their title.

The recent layoffs aren't just cost-cutting—they're a signal that organizations are rethinking how leadership works. The future isn't about choosing sides between ICs and managers. It's about building organizations where technical excellence and strategic leadership reinforce each other.

When you combine this with credit-based allocation and bounty systems, the opportunity becomes clear: we're creating a world where the best ideas win, where leadership is fluid, and where everyone can contribute their unique strengths to building amazing things.
