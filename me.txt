My name is Ziyad Mir. I was born on September 30th, 1991. My older brother was born two years before me. When I was about 3.5 years old, my family relocated to Toronto, Canada. My second brother was born there. My dad worked as a mechanical engineer, largely for energy companies and ended up at Bruce Power, and we moved eventually to Port Elgin, ON. My mother was a trained biochemist who became a full-time parent to raise our family. She later returned to work in customer service management.

Early Childhood
I don't remember much about my early years, but I have a few distinct memories. I remember attending a school with a diverse international curriculum. I don't recall much about the classes themselves, but I have a couple of memories that have stayed with me. One is of a kind teacher who would help me find the bus - perhaps I kept forgetting where to catch it, but I remember her holding my hand and guiding me. The other memory involves a talent show or presentation where I was either in the audience or participating. Life was quite hazy during that period - I was very young, just 3.5 years old when we relocated, my older brother was 5.5, and my younger brother wasn't born yet - my mom must have been pregnant with him near the time we moved.

My father worked at a beautiful resort complex near the coast. We'd be in a room during the day with my mom, and she'd teach my older brother and I the alphabet, how to read, and some simple math. She was my first real teacher. I remember us studying during daylight hours, partially because my mom valued education and partially because of the climate. When the sun set, I remember slamming shut our books and running outside down a long paved walkway to the swimming pools. The pools were near a shoreline, and we could see an island in the distance across the water.

High School
In high school, I played a lot of sports, with football (qb) and basketball (small forward) being my favourite. I also played rugby (fullback or wing) and soccer (right defence). I was pretty popular in high school, but it was kinda at a shallow surface level. I think this is because I moved to Port Elgin right before high school started, and it's a small town where everyone grew up together, so I didn't have the same times to everyone as the rest of the kids at my school. My first day of high school, I was feeling bold and showed up with a pink collared shirt on. I had a thin little moustache as well. I remember one moment from 9th grade pretty vividly. On the first day of school I had gym class and my teacher, Pete Buttenaar did some random role call and admin stuff with us — I got the sense that he was a no nonsense hard nosed former athlete. On the second day of gym class we went to the weight room and a huge (probably 6'1, 210) kid in my class said to Pete that he could probably bench 200 lbs. Pete laughed and said I bet you $100 you can't. Ronnie was strong and did it right then, one rep of 200 lbs at around 13 years old — I was pretty shocked but didn't fully understand since I had never bench pressed anything like that and didn't know what it meant. A few weeks later, we were playing football for the first time in class. Nobody really knew who I was, other than a couple of the athletic kids who saw me in the summer before school. I remember playing safety and immediately picking the ball off on a deep ball in a jump ball between me and two of the wide receivers. Pete was impressed and told me to come out for football next fall to play for his team (he was the football coach as well). I was cocky and said, yeah maybe, let's see. Pete said, "let's see? What else are you going to be down" and we both laughed. After that, I basically was part of the cool kids, who were mostly the athletic kids with a few of their party friends thrown in. One time in the main hall we were all chilling in between class or at lunch, and one a kid named Paul LaTouf came up to me and we were talking. He was about my weight and height, about 5'11 and maybe 175 lbs, athletic looking. You know how some people just have that aggressiveness to them, that real dog in them? That was him — his older brother was a bigger, stronger, meaner version of him as well (later I learned that their dad was exactly the same, and was a police officer or lieutenant at the Nuclear power plant where my dad worked). Anyways, Paul just kept talking in my face and I didn't take intimidation from anyone. He reached out to push me with both hands on my biceps but I reacted right away and pushed him back — it was like a standstill in a tug of war and I think he was pretty shocked at how strong I was. He was a big, strong kid. But so was I, and it might have been the first time he'd ever come face to face with someone who could kick his ass just as likely as he could kick mine. He never tested me again, in fact nobody in high school ever did after that.
