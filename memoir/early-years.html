Early Childhood Years

1991-1995 · Formative Experiences
Childhood First Memories Family

I was born on September 30th, 1991. My parents came from families with diverse experiences, and their own early professional lives involved seeking opportunities internationally. This path was taken by many professionals of their generation looking to build careers.

My father, with his mechanical engineering degree, found work in the energy sector. My mother, trained as a biochemist, set aside her career to focus on raising our family. My older brother was born in 1989, and I came along two years later.

Fragmented Memories

My memories from this early period are more like vivid sensory impressions than coherent narratives. I recall the quality of light – the harsh brightness of midday and the golden glow of late afternoon. I remember the smell of my mother's cooking, a comforting and familiar aroma.

I remember attending a school that catered to an international community, offering an education that maintained connections to diverse cultural heritages even while living abroad.

A teacher there was particularly kind and would help me find the bus. The warmth of her hand and the feeling of safety she provided are oddly clear in my memory, even as the details around it have faded.

Another school memory involves a talent show or a similar event. I recall music, bright colors, and a sense of excitement that felt almost overwhelming to my young mind.

Life was quite hazy around that time. We were young; I was 3.5 when we eventually moved to Canada, my older brother was 5.5, and my younger brother wasn’t born yet. The decision to emigrate was driven by my parents' desire for different opportunities and educational possibilities for their children.

My First Teacher

My father worked for a company located near the coast. The setting provided an interesting backdrop for my earliest years.

During the day, my mother would teach my older brother and me the alphabet, how to read, and some simple math. She was, in essence, my first real teacher. Looking back, I realize how she channeled her scientific training into creating structured learning experiences for us, using everyday objects as learning tools.

We often studied during daylight hours, partly because my mother was keen on our learning, and partly because the climate encouraged indoor activities during the hottest parts of the day. Our home became a classroom and playground during these hours.

Evening Adventures

When the sun set, I remember the joy of finishing our books and running outside to play. The sudden freedom after hours of indoor activities was exhilarating. The air would cool, and the entire atmosphere would transform.

I recall pools nearby, and the sea visible in the distance. The contrast between the maintained environment and the vastness of the natural world was striking.

These evening adventures were probably my earliest experiences of freedom and joy, creating a pattern of anticipation for what comes after necessary work is done. There's something about that rhythm—constraint followed by release, study followed by play—that has stayed with me throughout my life, shaping how I approach both work and leisure.

Cultural Influences

Growing up in an international environment exposed me to a variety of influences from a young age. This upbringing involved experiencing different customs and traditions, both within our household and in the broader expatriate community, contributing to a multifaceted perspective early on.

Though I was too young to fully appreciate it then, this early exposure to multiple cultures, languages, and perspectives likely laid the groundwork for the adaptability and openness to different viewpoints that have served me well throughout my career in diverse environments.

When we eventually moved to Toronto when I was 3.5 years old, we brought these cultural influences with us, adding yet another layer to our family's identity as we began to integrate into Canadian society. My early years, fleeting as they were in my memory, represent the first chapter in what would become a truly global life journey.
