Early Years in Saudi Arabia

1991-1995 · Jeddah
Childhood First Memories Family

I was born on September 30th, 1991 in Jeddah, Saudi Arabia. My parents are originally from Srinagar, Kashmir. Their journey from Kashmir to Saudi Arabia was part of a larger migration of South Asian professionals seeking opportunities in the growing Gulf economies of the 1980s.

My father, with his mechanical engineering degree, found work in Saudi Arabia's expanding energy sector. My mother, trained as a biochemist, set aside her career to focus on raising our family. My older brother was born in 1989, and I came along two years later.

Fragmented Memories

I don't remember much about living in Saudi Arabia, but the memories I do have are vivid sensory impressions rather than coherent narratives. I remember the quality of light - the harsh brightness of midday and the golden glow of late afternoon. I remember the smell of spices from my mother's cooking mixing with the salt air from the Red Sea.

I remember going to a school, which I later learned was called the "Indian Embassy School", which I think is now called the "International Indian School Jeddah". It was one of the few schools catering to the expatriate Indian community, offering education that maintained connections to our cultural heritage even as we lived thousands of miles from India.

My teacher was really nice and she used to help me find the bus — maybe I kept forgetting where I go to catch the bus, but I remember that I had a teacher who would hold my hand and then help me either figure out how to get to the bus stop or maybe walk me there. The warmth of her hand and the feeling of safety she provided are oddly clear in my memory, even as the details around it have faded.

The other memory of school is that there was a talent show or a show and tell or something like that, and I remember either watching it or maybe being on stage. There was music, bright colors, and a sense of excitement that felt almost overwhelming to my young mind.

Life was really hazy around that time. We were young; I was 3.5 when we left, my older brother was 5.5, and my younger brother wasn't born yet — my mom must have been pregnant with him near the time we left for Canada. The decision to emigrate was driven by my parents' desire for different opportunities and educational possibilities for their children.

My First Teacher

My dad worked at a place called Durrat-Al-Arus, which I remember as a sort of resort on the coast of Jeddah. It was a beautiful complex of buildings that blended traditional Arabian architectural elements with modern amenities. The contrast between our home life and the resort where my father worked created an interesting backdrop for my earliest years.

We'd be in a room during the day with my mom, and she'd teach my older brother and I the alphabet, and how to read, and some simple math. I guess she was my first real teacher. Looking back, I realize how she channeled her scientific training into creating structured learning experiences for us, using everyday objects as learning tools.

I remember us studying when it was daylight, partially because my mom wanted us to learn, partially because it was too hot to be outside, and partially because what else do you do as a woman in Jeddah with two young boys. The constraints on women's activities in Saudi Arabia meant that our home became a classroom, playground, and world unto itself during the daytime hours.

Evening Adventures

When the sun set, I remember slamming shut our books and running outside and down a long paved walkway to the pools. The sudden freedom after hours of confinement indoors was exhilarating. The air would cool dramatically after sunset, and the entire atmosphere would transform from oppressive to inviting.

I recall the pools being right by the coast, and we could see an island somewhere in the distance in the Red Sea. The contrast between the carefully maintained blue of the swimming pools and the wild, darker blue of the sea beyond created a boundary between the controlled environment of the resort and the vast unknown of the natural world.

These evening adventures were probably my earliest experiences of freedom and joy, creating a pattern of anticipation for what comes after the necessary work is done. There's something about that rhythm—constraint followed by release, study followed by play—that has stayed with me throughout my life, shaping how I approach both work and leisure.

Cultural Influences

Growing up in Saudi Arabia as a child of Kashmiri parents created a rich, multilayered cultural identity from the very beginning. At home, we spoke Urdu and English, ate traditional Kashmiri food, and observed Muslim traditions. Outside, we were immersed in Saudi Arabian culture and the international atmosphere of the expatriate community.

Though I was too young to fully appreciate it then, this early exposure to multiple cultures, languages, and perspectives likely laid the groundwork for the adaptability and openness to different viewpoints that have served me well throughout my career in diverse environments like Silicon Valley.

When we eventually moved to Toronto when I was 3.5 years old, we brought these cultural influences with us, adding yet another layer to our family's identity as we began to integrate into Canadian society. My early years in Saudi Arabia, fleeting as they were in my memory, represent the first chapter in what would become a truly global life journey.
